Introduction

As you prepare to plan your wedding liturgy, there are a few pieces of Catholic theology that are important to keep in mind. These have to do with the nature of the Sacrament, your role as the couple, and a few general liturgical principles.

Once you have read this background info, it will be time to make your first choice: what form of the wedding liturgy will you celebrate? There are three options, and they are all valid.

NoteThree forms of celebrating Matrimony

In the Catholic Church, matrimony can be celebrated in three ways:

  • the celebration of the Sacrament within Mass,
  • the celebration of the Sacrament in its own separate liturgy,
  • or the celebration of a non-sacramental marriage between a Catholic and their unbaptized partner.

Each form has its own section on this website.

To help decide which option you will celebrate, take a look at each form’s respective page: Matrimony within Mass, Matrimony without Mass, and Matrimony with a Catechumen or Non-Christian.

Catholic beliefs about marriage

In the Catholic Church, marriage is considered to be a privileged way of living out the universal call to love of God and neighbor. It exists to help each partner grow in faith, hope, and love, and by so doing to serve the Church and the world. In other words, the way that you live a life of love and commitment with your spouse will be foundational to the way in which you work toward Christ’s reign of peace and justice in the new creation.

The marriage itself consists in the consent between two spouses, exchanged once and intended to last the rest of their lives. When two baptized Christians get married, their exchange of consent is considered to be a Sacrament.

NoteThe difference between vows and consent

It is common for couples in the United States to refer to their “marriage vows.” In the Catholic Church, there is actually no such thing!

For the purpose of Catholic liturgy, a vow is something one makes to God, e.g., by making religious vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience. Consent, on the other hand, is different because it is between two human persons. Using different language highlights that matrimonial consent is a mutual endeavor. It is a shared commitment two people make to each other in the sight of God and the community.

At this point, you might be wondering what you are consenting to! A Catholic marriage consents to four things, and relies on God’s immense generosity to provide them as gifts. These are sometimes called the goods of marriage:

  • Unity refers to the closeness and completeness of the bond between two spouses. As the scripture says, “they are no longer two, but one flesh” (Matt 19:6). You are consenting to share your whole lives and all of who you are as one.

  • Indissolubility or permanence refers to the lifelong nature of a commitment to marriage. Marital love is generous, faithful, and filled with hope. While it is a sad reality that some marriages lead to divorce or separation, this is never the intent.

  • Fidelity refers to the intention two people express to be faithful to each other in all things. By living a life of fidelity to your spouse, you represent and give witness to God’s faithfulness to the people of God.

  • Fruitfulness refers to the nature of love itself, which cannot be contained within one partnership. Rather, true love always overflows into the life of the family, the church, and the world. “A good tree cannot bear bad fruit, nor can a rotten tree bear good fruit” (Matt 7:18). As part of your commitment to fruitfulness, you also express your openness to raising children of your own, should that be God’s will.

TipA brief note from Pope Francis

The love of friendship is called “charity” when it perceives and esteems the “great worth” of another person. Beauty…enables us to appreciate the sacredness of a person, without feeling the need to possess it. In a consumerist society, the sense of beauty is impoverished and so joy fades. Everything is there to be purchased, possessed, or consumed, including people. Tenderness, on the other hand, is a sign of a love free of selfish possessiveness. It makes us approach a person with immense respect and a certain dread of causing them harm or taking away their freedom. Loving another person involves the joy of contemplating and appreciating their innate beauty and sacredness, which is greater than my needs. This enables me to seek their good even when they cannot belong to me, or when they are no longer physically appealing but intrusive and annoying. For “the love by which one person is pleasing to another depends on his or her giving something freely.” -Amoris Laetitia, 127.

Your role as the couple

One of the coolest things about Catholic marriage is that the two spouses are the ministers of the Sacrament! Nobody else can make you be married. It is your own free, loving, and mutual consent that makes the marriage. This is what God blesses as a Sacrament.

The importance of your role as ministers of the Sacrament will be emphasized through various signs and symbols during your wedding liturgy, such as the way in which you enter the church, where you sit, how you exchange consent, and other liturgical roles you might take on.

The roles of the other ministers

As with any public celebration of the Church, many other ministers are involved:

  • Either a priest or deacon normally presides. Only a priest can preside if you are celebrating Mass. Aside from their other duties, such as preaching and leading prayer, the presider’s role is to represent your community, the Church, in witnessing your exchange of consent.
NoteWhy say ‘presider’ instead of ‘celebrant’?

You will note that I usually refer the the priest or deacon who leads the liturgy as the presider. I do this for a few reasons.

  1. First, especially when celebrating Matrimony without Mass, the priest or deacon, while necessary, is not the minister of the Sacrament. You are! Of course, the priest is still considered to be the minister of the Eucharist.

  2. Second, because either a priest or deacon (or in some places even a non-ordained minister) can preside at a Catholic wedding outside of Mass, it is easier to refer to their role as ‘presider’ than to always mention each kind of minister that could fulfill that role.

  3. Perhaps most importantly, to call the priest or deacon the ‘celebrant’ draws the focus away from the assembly that celebrates as one. Calling the minister a ‘presider’ emphasizes that this role only makes sense in relationship to a community that has gathered for prayer. It is always the faith of the Church that effects a Sacrament, not the actions of one person, ordained or otherwise.

  • Especially within Mass, a deacon can assist the priest and preach the homily. With good pastoral reason and permission of the pastor, the deacon may also witness the consent even when a priest is present.

    • Similarly, a priest concelebrant may take some of these roles as well.

  • Lectors proclaim the scripture readings during the liturgy of the word. If there is no deacon, they may also announce the intentions of the universal prayer (the prayers of the faithful).

  • A cantor and/or psalmist lead the assembly in their sung responses during the celebration. They are often supported by additional music ministers such as instrumentalists, a music director, and/or a choir.

  • Altar servers assist the couple and the presider in the sanctuary. Having two or more servers allows for additional ritual actions during the liturgy, such as the use of incense.

  • It is common in the United States to include a ring bearer and/or a flower girl.

  • If you are celebrating Mass, you might wish to select a couple or family to carry the gifts in an offertory procession. See the note about options for the offertory at Offertory procession.

  • If you do not have enough priests and/or deacons to distribute communion, you may select as many extraordinary ministers of Holy Communion as necessary.

Your choice of ministers might reflect the role these people play in your lives, the lives of your family and/or community, and the desired solemnity of the liturgy.

General liturgical principles

Any public celebration of the Church is always God’s act first. It is God who created us, endowed us with free will, and marked us as members of the body of Christ at our Baptism. In the case of Matrimony, it is God who plants the seeds of love in a couple’s hearts and who nurtures those seeds as they blossom into a lifelong bond. It is also God who will bless your desire to respond to that love by providing the grace you need throughout your married life.

Second, any public celebration of the Church is just that: a celebration of the gathered community in union with the wider community, which is the Church spread throughout the world and through history. The Roman Missal, which contains the instructions and prayers for the celebration of Mass, begins with the words, “populo congregato,” i.e., “the people having gathered.” Note that nothing is said about the priest or any other ministers! Having people gather is the first and most important prerequisite for a valid liturgical celebration.

One way to reflect the importance of the gathered assembly is to express the diversity of ministries in the liturgy. In other words, more people helping (within reason) is a good thing! Having a sufficient number and variety of ministers exercising their roles in a prayerful manner better represents that the celebration is an act of the believing community. Another important way to express this value is by designing a liturgy that will facilitate the full, conscious, and active participation of the entire community.

TipA note about liturgy from Vatican II

Mother Church earnestly desires that all the faithful should be led to that fully conscious, and active participation in liturgical celebrations which is demanded by the very nature of the liturgy. Such participation by the Christian people as “a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a redeemed people” (1 Pet 2:9; cf. 2:4-5), is their right and duty by reason of their baptism. In the restoration and promotion of the sacred liturgy, this full and active participation by all the people is the aim to be considered before all else; for it is the primary and indispensable source from which the faithful are to derive the true Christian spirit. -Sacrosanctum Concilium, 14.

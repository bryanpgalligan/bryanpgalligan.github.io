5  Liturgy of the Eucharist

Now that you are married, you will prepare to celebrate and receive Communion in the Body and Blood of Christ for the first time as a couple.

5.1 Offertory procession

The intended symbolism of the offertory procession is that the community that celebrates presents gifts to nourish their own life and worship in community and to serve the needs of the poor as an outgrowth of that same life and worship. This usually takes place in the form of bread and wine, which will be consecrated as the Body and Blood of Christ, and money, which supports the operations of the Church and some charitable cause, such as a local soup kitchen or advocacy group. This symbolism is why the gifts are brought up in a procession from the midst of the assembly.

Celebrating Matrimony within Mass provides another option. Here, the newly married couple may bring the bread and wine to the altar. This shows a unity between the two Sacraments, and provides the couple with an opportunity to represent the community that just witnessed their Marriage.

TipThe spiritual meaning of the offertory

The bringing of the gifts to the altar is a ritual gesture, first Jewish and later Christian, in which three elements interact simultaneously and never independently: the faithful who offer, the gifts that are placed on the altar of the Lord, and the poor with whom the goods of creation are shared. -Goffredo Boselli, The Spiritual Meaning of the Liturgy, 80.

You may choose to have an ordinary offertory procession, to bring the bread and wine to the altar yourself, or to have no offertory procession. The choice depends on which symbols you believe will most fully express the deeper meaning of the liturgy as an action of the community that celebrates.

5.2 The preface

The preface is the first part of the Eucharistic Prayer. It is said or sung by the priest right after the introductory dialogue (“The Lord be with you…Lift up your hearts…Let us give thanks to the Lord our God”). The preface is always concluded with the Sanctus (Holy, holy, holy).

Three options exist for the preface.

5.2.1 Option 1: The dignity of the Marriage covenant

It is truly right and just, our duty and our salvation, always and everywhere to give you thanks, Lord, holy Father, almighty and eternal God.

For you have forged the covenant of Marriage as a sweet yoke of harmony and an unbreakable bond of peace, so that the chaste and fruitful love of holy Matrimony may serve to increase the children you adopt as your own.

By your providence and grace, O Lord, you accomplish the wonder of this twofold design: that, while the birth of children brings beauty to the world, their rebirth in Baptism gives increase to the Church, through Christ our Lord.

Through him, with the Angels and all the Saints, we sing the hymn of your praise, as without end we acclaim:

5.2.2 Option 2: The great Sacrament of Matrimony

It is truly right and just, our duty and our salvation, always and everywhere to give you thanks, Lord, holy Father, almighty and eternal God, through Christ our Lord.

For in him you have made a new covenant with your people, so that, as you have redeemed man and woman by the mystery of Christ’s Death and Resurrection, so in Christ you might make them partakers of divine nature and joint heirs with him of heavenly glory.

In the union of husband and wife you give a sign of Christ’s loving gift of grace, so that the Sacrament we celebrate might draw us back more deeply into the wondrous design of your love.

And so, with the Angels and all the Saints, we praise you, and without end we acclaim:

5.2.3 Option 3: Matrimony as a sign of divine love

It is truly right and just, our duty and our salvation, always and everywhere to give you thanks, Lord, holy Father, almighty and eternal God.

For you willed that the human race, created by the gift of your goodness, should be raised to such high dignity that in the union of husband and wife you might bestow a true image of your love.

For those you created out of charity you call to the law of charity without ceasing and grant them a share in your eternal charity.

And so, the Sacrament of holy Matrimony, as the abiding sign of your own love, consecrates the love of man and woman, through Christ our Lord.

Through him, with the Angels and all the Saints, we sing the hymn of your praise, as without end we acclaim:

5.3 The Eucharistic Prayer

Any time Marriage is celebrated during Mass, the couple’s names are included in the Eucharistic Prayer. The text for this special commemoration depends on the Eucharistic Prayer chosen. Again, you have three options.

TipThe spiritual meaning of the breaking of the bread

It is not only as bread (substance) but as bread broken and shared (relation), that the eucharistic bread fully realizes its essence and the truth of the Eucharist. Today more than ever, the fractio panis is the great prophetic gesture by which the church proclaims that the Lord has entrusted to humanity the resources of the earth and that those who have abundance must share with the poor in solidarity and justice. -Goffredo Boselli, The Spiritual Meaning of the Liturgy, 186.

5.3.1 Eucharistic Prayer I

Eucharistic Prayer I, aka the Roman Canon, was the only Eucharistic Prayer in use in the centuries immediately before the Second Vatican Council. It is the longest of the three prayers and includes the most gestures on the part of the priest. The text of the commemoration is:

Therefore, Lord, we pray: graciously accept this oblation of our service, the offering of your servants N. and N. and of your whole family, who entreat your majesty on their behalf; and as you have brought them to their wedding day, so (gladden them with your gift of the children they desire and) bring them in your kindness to the length of days for which they hope.

The text in parentheses can be omitted.

5.3.2 Eucharistic Prayer II

Eucharistic Prayer II is the shortest of the three options, and is often used at daily Masses throughout the week. The text of the commemoration is:

Be mindful also, Lord, of N. and N., whom you have brought to their wedding day, so that by your grace they may abide in mutual love and in peace.

5.3.3 Eucharistic Prayer III

Eucharistic Prayer III is probably the most common choice for wedding liturgies. It is based on ancient texts of prayer from the first centuries of Christianity. The commemoration is:

Strengthen, we pray, in the grace of Marriage N. and N., whom you have brought happily to their wedding day, that under your protection they may always be faithful in their lives to the covenant they have sealed in your presence. In your compassion, O merciful Father, gather to yourself all your children scattered throughout the world.

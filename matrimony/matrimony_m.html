3  Celebration of Matrimony

This is it! The moment in the liturgy when you will actually be married! The Celebration of Matrimony immediately follows the homily.

The first thing that will happen are the Questions Before the Consent. The priest will ask you if you have come of your own free will, if you are prepared to love and honor each other for as long as you both shall live, and if you are prepared to accept children and raise them in the faith. You will both respond, “I have,” “I am,” and “I am.” Then, you will declare your consent.

TipA note from Pope Benedict XVI

The matrimony is a Gospel in itself, a Good News for the world today…The union of a man and a woman, their becoming “one flesh” in charity, in fruitful and indissoluble love, is a sign that speaks of God with a force and an eloquence which in our days has become greater…And it is not by chance. Marriage is linked to faith, but not in a general way. Marriage, as a union of faithful and indissoluble love, is based upon the grace that comes from the triune God, who in Christ loved us with a faithful love, even to the Cross. -Homily on October 7, 2012 in St. Peter’s Square

3.1 The consent

In U.S. culture, it is common for the officiant at a wedding to obtain the consent of the bride and groom through questioning, e.g., “Shrek, do you take Fiona to be your lawfully wedded wife…” This is the moment at which the bride and groom will say, “I do.”

In the Catholic Church, the preferred way to declare consent is for the bride and groom to actually say what they are consenting to! As with other symbols in the liturgy, this emphasizes that the couple are the ministers of the Sacrament, and that it is their action of mutual consent that makes the Marriage, not some magic spell cast by a priest or deacon. Consent can still be obtained through questioning if necessary, but this is not ideal as it places too much focus on the priest or deacon at precisely the moment when the couple are celebrating the Sacrament we all gathered to witness.

You may choose from two forms for the consent. Either form can be adapted into questioning if you prefer.

3.1.1 First form

The bridegroom says:

I, N., take you, N. to be my wife. I promise to be faithful to you, in good times and in bad, in sickness and in health, to love you and to honor you all the days of my life.

The bride says:

I, N., take you, N. to be my husband. I promise to be faithful to you, in good times and in bad, in sickness and in health, to love you and to honor you all the days of my life.

3.1.2 Second form

The bridegroom says:

I, N., take you, N., for my lawful wife, to have and to hold, from this day forward, for better, for worse, for richer, for poorer, in sickness and in health, to love and to cherish until death do us part.

The bride says:

I, N., take you, N., for my lawful husband, to have and to hold, from this day forward, for better, for worse, for richer, for poorer, in sickness and in health, to love and to cherish until death do us part.

And that’s it! You’re married! The priest will say some words to receive your consent and invite the whole assembly to praise God, saying “Let us bless the Lord,” to which all respond, “Thanks be to God.”

3.2 Rings and Arras

Now that you are married, the liturgy includes some explanatory symbols to unpack the Sacrament you just celebrated. The first is the blessing and giving of the rings.

In some cultures it is also common to celebrate the blessing and giving of the arras (coins). If you would like to include that as part of your celebration, it will happen here.
